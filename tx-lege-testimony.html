Testifying before the Texas Legislature

Thank you for taking the time to testify before the Texas legislature! Itâ€™s not an easy breezy process, but do not be deterred. The information here will help prepare you. You’ll rock it.

You can give spoken and/or written testimony at public hearings. You can also simply register your position on a bill without giving testimony, and your position will go into the public record.

Written vs. Oral Testimony

Not all committees will disseminate written testimony if you do not also give spoken testimony. The best way to find this out is to call the committee and speak to the clerk in charge of taking testimony for that committee.

If you do not want to speak at a hearing but want to ensure that legislators receive a written testimony, you may:

  1. Give a copy of your written testimony to the clerk for that committee.
  2. Call the offices of the legislators on the committee hearing the relevant bill, find out who are the legislative aides working on that bill, and send a copy of your testimony to each aide.

Preparing your written statement

Begin your statement by addressing the committee and identifying yourself, if you’re representing yourself or an organization, the bill you’re testifying about, and your stance on the bill. For example:

“Mr. (or Madam) Chair, and members of the committee, my name is Awesome Dude from Austin and I’m representing myself (or Super Cool Org) to testify in favor of (or in opposition to) HB/SB #.”

The rest of your statement should be a clear, direct, and concise as possible. Bullet point style would not be a poor choice. If you can and feel comfortable doing so, making your statement as personal as possible is highly encouraged. If you reference numbers or statistics, include a citation. You should never be rude or threaten any of the committee members.

Preparing your spoken statement

For most bills, you’re allowed three minutes to speak, but for some you only get two. Begin your testimony by identifying yourself, who you may represent, and your stance on the bill. Continue with a concise, and if you wish personal statement about your position. If public speaking makes you nervous, it is best to practice a couple of times before the hearing. You might find it useful to write down some key points on an index card and ad-lib off those.

You may be asked questions during or after your testimony, so it’s also a good idea to write down some notes, statistics, citations, or even full answers to questions you are likely to be asked.

Preparation Checklist

  • Prepare a written statement 2-3 minutes in length
  • Print 20 copies of your written statement. Make sure your name is on each one.
  • Practice your statement
  • Write down the information you’ll need at the hearing, including:
    • Bill number
    • Name of committee the bill will be heard in
    • The room number where the hearing will take place
    • The room number of the overflow room
  • Write down any notes you want to have on hand for questions.

How to register to give testimony

You can only register to testify at a public hearing on the day of the hearing. Witness registration opens on the day the bill is being heard at 8:00 AM. Usually, registration closes after the bill has been heard, but be aware that some committees have closed registration early this session, notably for SB 4 (Sanctuary Cities bill).

To register, use the computer kiosks located near the public hearing room. Note that some public hearings take place in other buildings in the Capitol Complex, where there are also registration kiosks. You can also register from your own mobile device as long as you are connected to the Capitol’s wifi.

To see this process in action, watch the House’s video on how to use the kiosks.

Senate registration is similar.

Also note that hearings for some bills have different rules. For example, anyone testifying for SB 6 had to fill out a paper registration card instead of signing up electronically. If you’re not sure what the process is or have any questions about it, call the committee. If you get lost or confused the day off, find someone to ask, such as a security guard or legislative staff.

What to expect on the day of

If submitting a written statement or written copies of a spoken statement, bring 20 copies of the document with you to the public hearing. Make sure your name is on each copy. The day of the hearing, bring those copies with and any other notes you want to have on hand with you.

Near the room in which the public hearing is taking place, there will be a kiosk of computers. You will use one of these computers to register your position on the bill and, if you want, sign up for testimony. If submitting written testimony, give copies of your statement to the committee clerk. You may then go on your merry way. If you decide to speak, after signing in, go to the hearing room or the designated overflow room and wait for your name to be called.

Once your name is called, proceed to the hearing room. If you made copies of your spoken statement, hand them to the clerk (who will then give them to each member of the committee) then sit at the designated table and give your testimony. If you submit copies of your spoken statement, it is best not to read the statement word for word as legislators can read it themselves. Instead, address the key points you want to raise.

When giving your testimony, you will be seated at a table in front of a semi-circle of committee members. There will be a microphone, stop clock, and buzzer on the table. When you have one minute left to speak, you will hear a beep. The second beep means you are out of time, at which point you should wrap up your statement quickly. Committee members may respond to your testimony with questions.

While the exchange is supposed to be courteous and professional, it is not uncommon for legislators to behave in disrespectful and/or dismissive ways towards speakers whose viewpoints different from theirs. If this happens, stay calm. Don’t allow yourself to be rattled.

It is not always clear how long you will have to wait to speak as this depends on how many bills are being considered at a hearing, the order the bills are being considered in, how many people have signed up to testify, and whether or not open public testimony on a bill is preceded by invited testimony. Bring a water bottle and snacks.

Finally, it is best to dress business casual. If you are representing an organization, especially if you are going as a group, it is not uncommon for everyone to wear official T-shirts for that organization or a particular color (e.g. purple for transgender rights).

Parking

Free 2-hour parking can be found in the Capitol’s visitor parking garage on 13th and San Jacinto Streets. The visitor’s garage can get full fast, so it’s best to get there early. You can also use street parking and other garages around the Capitol.
